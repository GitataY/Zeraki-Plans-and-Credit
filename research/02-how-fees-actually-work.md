# How School Fees Actually Work in Kenya

## The Official Process

On paper, paying school fees is straightforward:

1. School sets a fee structure at the start of the year (approved by the Board of Management)
2. Parents receive a fee breakdown - usually printed on the admission letter or report card
3. Fees are due on the first day of term
4. Parent pays via the school's M-Pesa paybill (using the student's registration number as the account number) or by bank deposit
5. School issues a receipt
6. If fees aren't paid, the school follows up

In reality, almost nothing works like this.

## What Actually Happens: Term Opening Week

### Day 1: The Sorting

School opens. Students arrive. The bursar has a printed list of everyone's fee balance from last term plus the new term's charges. Some balances carry forward - a parent who didn't clear last term still owes that plus this term's amount.

The first task is sorting who's paid and who hasn't. In schools using Zeraki Finance, this is digital - the bursar can see balances on their phone. In schools without a system, it's a ledger book and a stack of M-Pesa confirmations forwarded via SMS.

Typically on Day 1:

- 20–35% of parents have paid in full
- 10–20% have paid something (partial)
- 50–70% have paid nothing yet

### Days 2–5: The Pressure Builds

The headteacher reviews the collection numbers. They're running a business - teachers need salaries (often due mid-month), the kitchen needs food supplies, the landlord wants rent. The gap between what's in the account and what's needed is real and immediate.

The headteacher makes a decision: which students to send home.

This decision is rarely systematic. It's a mix of:

- **Who owes the most** - students with large arrears get targeted first
- **Who the headteacher knows** - parents who've built a relationship may get more grace
- **How badly the school needs cash** - desperate schools are more aggressive
- **Which parents are likely to respond** - some parents pay when their child is sent home, others don't, and the school learns who's who over time

### Days 5–10: The Negotiations

Children are sent home with notes. Or the bursar calls parents directly. Or the child simply isn't allowed into class until the parent comes to "see the headteacher."

This is where the informal arrangement system begins. It happens in the headteacher's office, at the school gate, or over a phone call. It's always verbal. It's always one-on-one. And it follows a predictable script:

**The parent says:**

- "I'll pay by Friday" (the most common)
- "I'm waiting for my salary / my husband's salary / money from upcountry"
- "Let me pay half now and the rest by end of month"
- "I have a job coming next week, I'll bring it then"
- "Please, just give me two more weeks"

**The headteacher/bursar says:**

- "OK, but if you don't pay by [date], we'll have to send them home again"
- "Pay at least something today to show good faith"
- "We can't keep doing this every term, you need to plan better"
- Sometimes: "Fine, bring what you can, we'll work it out"
- Sometimes: "No. Not until you pay."

**The agreement - if there is one is:**

- Verbal only
- Recorded nowhere (or in the bursar's personal notebook)
- Based on trust and the parent's track record at that school
- Enforced by the threat of sending the child home again
- Forgotten if the bursar is busy or if a different staff member handles the follow-up

### Weeks 2–8: The Trickle

Fees trickle in. Some parents pay the full amount in Week 2 after scraping together money. Others pay in 3–4 chunks over the term. A few pay nothing until the very end when they need the child's report card (some schools withhold report cards for unpaid fees).

The bursar's job during this period is part accountant, part debt collector. They send SMS reminders (manually, or through Zeraki Finance's bulk SMS), make phone calls, and report to the headteacher on the collection gap.

### End of Term: The Reconciliation

By end of term, 85–95% of fees have typically been collected. The remaining 5–15% either:

- Rolls over to next term (added to the new balance)
- Gets written off (the school absorbs it)
- Becomes a reason to deny re-admission next term
- Gets partially recovered when the parent comes for the report card

## The People Involved

### The Bursar

The most important person in this system. They know every parent's pattern. They know who always pays in Week 1, who always needs a month, who always promises and never delivers. This knowledge is entirely in their head. When the bursar leaves or is replaced, the institutional memory disappears.

Zeraki Finance has partially digitised this - the bursar can see balances and payment history. But the **judgement calls** - who gets grace, who gets sent home, what arrangements to make - are still entirely human and informal.

### The Headteacher

Sets the enforcement policy. Some headteachers are strict ("no fees, no class"), others are lenient ("let them learn, we'll sort out the money"). This varies school to school and even term to term depending on the school's financial pressure.

### The Class Teacher

Often the messenger. Told to "send these students to the office" or "tell these students not to come tomorrow." The class teacher knows the impact on the child but has no authority over fee decisions.

### The Parent

Caught between wanting their child in school and not having the money right now. The negotiation at the school gate is one of the most stressful interactions in their financial life. Many parents describe feeling ashamed, judged, and desperate.

Parents who are too embarrassed to negotiate sometimes just keep their child at home until they have the full amount - choosing invisibility over confrontation. Their children miss weeks of school not because the school sent them home, but because the parent couldn't face the conversation.

### The Child

Knows exactly what's happening. Sees other children being sent home. Worries about being next. When it happens to them, they walk home in their uniform in the middle of the day - visible to neighbours, visible to other students. Some children hide the fact that they've been sent home, spending the day elsewhere and returning at normal hours.

The academic impact is obvious - missed lessons, missed exams, disrupted continuity. The psychological impact is harder to measure but consistently documented: shame, anxiety, withdrawal, reduced sense of belonging at school.

## What the Informal System Gets Right

It's important to acknowledge this isn't all broken:

- **It's flexible.** The headteacher can make exceptions for genuinely hard cases. A rigid system might reject a parent who's 50 shillings short.
- **It's relationship-based.** Parents who've built trust with the school get grace. This rewards consistent behaviour over time.
- **It works, eventually.** Most schools do collect 85–95% of fees by end of term. The money comes in - just slowly.

## What the Informal System Gets Wrong

- **It's invisible.** No records, no consistency, no accountability. Two parents in identical situations may get completely different treatment.
- **It's undignified.** The negotiation requires the parent to perform financial distress in front of school staff. This is especially hard for parents who are doing their best.
- **It punishes children for their parents' cash flow.** The child has no agency in the situation but bears the consequence.
- **It generates no useful data.** The bursar knows patterns from memory but can't analyse them, share them, or use them for planning. When the bursar changes, the knowledge is gone.
- **It can't scale.** A school with 500 students and 200 parents needing arrangements cannot run 200 individual verbal negotiations every term.

## Where Zeraki Plans Fits

The product doesn't replace the informal system. It **formalises the parts that should be systematic** while preserving the flexibility that makes the system work.

| What stays human                                        | What becomes systematic                                          |
| ------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Headteacher deciding the school's overall fee policy    | Instalment plan options and terms                                |
| Bursar having discretion for exceptional hardship cases | Tracking which parents are on plans and whether they're on track |
| Parent-school relationship and trust                    | Automated notifications when payments are due or missed          |
| Flexibility for genuine emergencies                     | Data on plan adherence across all parents                        |

The goal isn't to remove human judgement. It's to give the bursar a system so they can focus their human judgement on the cases that genuinely need it - instead of spending it on 150 repetitive conversations.
