# Risks and Mitigations

## Layer 1: Zeraki Plans

| # | Risk | Severity | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|------|----------|------------|------------|
| 1 | **Schools don't honour the "on a plan = stays in school" commitment.** The headteacher sends children home regardless of plan status because the school needs cash urgently. | High | Medium | This is the single biggest risk. Mitigation is primarily about school selection for the pilot: choose schools with headteachers who genuinely want to reduce sent-home incidents. Include the commitment in the school's onboarding agreement. Track and report on violations. Ultimately, the product cannot force school behaviour, only make the right behaviour easier. |
| 2 | **Low parent uptake.** Parents don't request plans because they don't trust the system, don't understand the SMS flow, or prefer to negotiate in person as they always have. | Medium | Medium | Path C (bursar-initiated plans) is the safety valve. Even if zero parents request plans via app or SMS, the bursar can still record every in-person arrangement in the system. This captures the value for the school (dashboard, tracking) even without parent-side adoption. Over time, parents who see the system working may shift to self-service. |
| 3 | **Bursar doesn't process the approval queue.** Plan requests pile up unapproved because the bursar is overwhelmed in Week 1 and doesn't check the queue. | Medium | High | Week 1 is the busiest time. Default to auto-approve for the pilot to reduce bursar workload. Send the bursar a daily summary SMS: "You have X plan requests pending. Open Zeraki Finance to review." Keep the approval flow to one tap per request, with an "Approve All" option. |
| 4 | **Plans create false confidence.** A parent selects a plan but then doesn't pay, and the school doesn't send the child home because the system shows "on a plan." By Week 6, the child has been in school the whole term and the parent owes the full amount. | High | Low | The dashboard flags parents who are "on a plan, not started" (selected a plan but made zero payments) and "on a plan, behind schedule." The bursar should follow up with these parents by Week 2. The overdue notification after 3 days gives early warning. If a parent misses 2 consecutive instalments, the system should escalate the alert. |
| 5 | **SMS delivery failures.** Fee balance SMS with plan options doesn't reach the parent due to wrong number, phone off, or carrier issues. Parent never knows plans exist. | Medium | Low | SMS delivery reports flag failures. Bursar sees which parents didn't receive the message. For critical messages (plan confirmation, overdue notices), consider a retry after 24 hours. The app is a backup channel for smartphone parents. |
| 6 | **Manual reconciliation bottleneck.** Cash-paying parents' plans show as overdue because the bursar hasn't recorded the payment yet. Parent gets an overdue SMS when they've already paid. | Medium | Medium | Design the overdue SMS to go out 3 days after the due date, not the same day. This gives the bursar time to record cash payments. Include a note in the overdue SMS: "If you've already paid, please disregard this message. Your school will update your records shortly." |
| 7 | **Parents game the system.** Parents request the longest plan with the smallest payments, knowing the school won't send their child home while a plan is active, even if they never intend to pay the full amount. | Medium | Low | The approval step exists for this reason. Bursars who know their parents can adjust terms or decline requests from parents with a history of broken commitments. Plan data across terms will reveal patterns (parent always selects a plan, never completes it), which the scoring model can incorporate. |
| 8 | **Data quality issues.** Payment matching fails because parents enter the wrong account number on M-Pesa, or the school's paybill reconciliation has gaps. Plans show incorrect status. | Medium | Medium | Unmatched payments should surface in a "needs review" queue for the bursar, not be silently dropped. The system should attempt fuzzy matching (e.g., account number off by one digit, parent phone number matches a known parent). Manual reconciliation is the fallback for anything the system can't match. |

## Layer 2: Zeraki Credit

| # | Risk | Severity | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|------|----------|------------|------------|
| 9 | **Paybill integration doesn't support the waterfall model.** Schools own their own M-Pesa paybills. For the waterfall to work, Zeraki needs to intercept incoming payments and forward the lender's share via B2B transfer. If the paybill provider doesn't support this, the entire Layer 2 repayment model breaks. | High | Medium | This is the highest-priority technical question. Options: (a) Zeraki integrates with major paybill providers to intercept and allocate, (b) schools move to a Zeraki-managed paybill (unlikely given resistance to change), (c) a different settlement model where the lender collects separately (breaks the invisible lender design). Must validate before build. Pilot with schools whose paybill setup allows B2B forwarding. |
| 10 | **Default rates are higher than expected.** Parents who qualified based on historical data default this term because the same life event that made them need credit also makes them unable to repay. | High | Low | The eligibility criteria are deliberately strict (4+ terms of history, 90%+ payment speed for Path 1; 3+ plan payments for Path 2). The lending partner also applies their own risk assessment on top of Zeraki's eligibility. Even so, some defaults are expected. Target is below 10%. If default rates exceed 15% in the pilot, revisit the eligibility thresholds. Keep individual credit amounts small (KES 3,000-10,000) so that each default has limited impact on the lender. |
| 11 | **Parents feel pressured to accept credit.** A parent receives a credit offer and feels they must accept because the school suggested it, even if they could manage without it. | Medium | Low | The offer is sent once with no follow-up. "No thanks" is a clear option. Declining has zero consequences. The SMS and app language is invitational ("you may qualify") not urgent ("act now"). The credit offer is never tied to the child's enrolment status. |
| 12 | **Lender has no direct collection lever if a parent stops paying entirely.** In the waterfall model, the lender only gets repaid when the parent pays to the school paybill. If the parent's child drops out, transfers, or the parent simply stops paying, the lender has no way to collect except CRB listing. | Medium | Low | Built into the lender's risk model from the start. The natural incentive is strong: as long as the child is enrolled, the parent keeps paying school fees, and the waterfall captures the lender's share. CRB listing is the enforcement backstop. Purpose-locked small amounts (KES 3,000-10,000) keep per-borrower exposure low. Lenders who operate in embedded models (like Pezesha with Twiga) already accept this dynamic. |
| 13 | **Credit becomes normalised.** Parents who can afford to pay start expecting credit every term because it's available, shifting from "emergency safety net" to "standard payment method." | Low | Medium | Eligibility resets each term. A parent who uses credit in Term 1 and then pays in full in Term 2 maintains eligibility. A parent who uses credit in Term 1 and needs it again in Term 2 still qualifies if their history supports it, but the system tracks repeat usage. If repeat usage exceeds 50% of credit recipients across 3+ terms, revisit the product positioning. |
| 14 | **Data sharing concerns.** Parents are uncomfortable with their fee payment data being shared with a credit partner, even with consent. | Medium | Medium | Consent is explicit, two-step (YES then CONFIRM for SMS), and explained in plain language (what data, who sees it, why). Parents can decline consent and simply not receive the credit offer. No data is shared before consent. The credit partner is referred to as "a credit partner" rather than named prominently - this reduces anxiety while remaining DPA-compliant. If consent rates are low, it signals trust issues that need addressing before scaling. |
| 15 | **Reputational risk to the school.** Parents or media perceive the school as "pushing loans on struggling parents" rather than helping them. | Medium | Low | The school controls everything: whether to enable credit and who to offer it to. The lending partner is invisible to parents - all communication comes from the school sender ID, framed as support. "Based on your payment history, you may qualify for fee support" is very different from "apply for a loan now." The word "loan" is never used in any school-side communication. If any negative perception emerges during the pilot, pause and reassess messaging. |
| 16a | **Cash/bank deposit payments create settlement friction.** When a parent pays cash, the bursar records it in Zeraki Finance, and the waterfall allocates the lender's share. But the cash is in the school's safe or bank account, not in M-Pesa. The school needs to forward the lender's share from their paybill, which may not have sufficient funds. | Medium | Medium | MVP: manual process where the bursar initiates the transfer. This adds work for the bursar, which conflicts with the "reduce workload" promise. Track how often this happens. If cash payments with active credit arrangements are common, automate the reconciliation in V2 or explore a sweep arrangement between the school's bank account and the settlement account. |
| 16b | **Zeraki handling money movement creates regulatory exposure.** The waterfall means Zeraki is splitting and forwarding payments between the school and the lending partner. This could classify Zeraki as a "payment facilitator" or "payment service provider" under CBK regulations, which would require licensing. | Medium | Low | Consult a Kenyan fintech lawyer before build. Structurally, Zeraki instructs the transfer but doesn't hold funds (the school's paybill holds them). The B2B transfer goes directly from the school to the lender. This is closer to "payment instruction" than "payment processing," but the distinction needs legal validation. If regulatory risk is too high, the alternative is the lender collecting directly (which breaks the invisible lender model). |
| 16c | **CRB listing happens through a channel the parent doesn't recognise.** A parent who defaults gets a CRB listing from a lending partner they've never interacted with. This could cause confusion or anger, undermining trust in the school and in Zeraki. | Medium | Low | The CRB warning SMS (sent at 21 days overdue, from the school sender ID) must clearly explain that unpaid amounts may be reported to a credit bureau. The consent flow at application explicitly mentions CRB reporting. If a parent is listed and contacts the school, the bursar can see the credit arrangement details and direct the parent to Zeraki support. The lending partner's name is disclosed in the consent flow and the "Learn more" section of the app, even though it's not prominent in day-to-day communications. |

## Cross-cutting Risks (Both Layers)

| # | Risk | Severity | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|------|----------|------------|------------|
| 16 | **Zeraki Finance downtime during critical periods.** The system goes down in Week 1 of term when plan requests, approvals, and payments are all peaking. | High | Low | Load test before the pilot term. Ensure the system can handle concurrent plan requests from multiple schools. Have a manual fallback process documented: bursars record plans on paper and enter them when the system is back. |
| 17 | **Feature increases bursar workload instead of reducing it.** The approval queue, manual reconciliation, credit monitoring, and new dashboard become additional tasks on top of existing work. | Medium | Medium | Measure bursar time carefully during the pilot. If bursars report the system adds more work than it saves, simplify: default to auto-approve, reduce notification frequency, and focus on the dashboard value rather than the approval workflow. The product must save time overall, or schools will stop using it. |
| 18 | **Economic downturn affects all parents simultaneously.** A recession, inflation spike, or other macro shock means more parents can't pay and the product's assumptions about "most parents eventually pay" break down. | High | Low | This risk exists with or without the product. Structured plans give schools better visibility into who can pay and who genuinely can't, which is more useful during a downturn than the current invisible informal system. In a severe downturn, schools may choose to disable credit offers and focus on Layer 1 plans to maintain structure. The lending partner will also tighten their approval criteria during downturns, which is a natural brake on credit volume. |
| 19 | **Privacy breach.** Parent fee payment data is exposed or misused, violating DPA 2019 and destroying trust. | High | Low | Standard data protection practices: access controls (only the school's bursar sees their parents' data), encryption in transit and at rest, DPA-compliant data processing agreements. Zeraki already handles sensitive academic data. Fee payment data should follow the same security posture. Data sharing with the lending partner requires explicit two-step consent and a formal data sharing agreement with retention and deletion clauses. The waterfall settlement layer adds a new data surface (payment allocation records) that must be included in the security posture. |
| 20 | **Product works but doesn't scale beyond pilot.** The 50 pilot schools succeed but broader rollout fails because other schools have different dynamics, less engaged bursars, or different parent populations. | Medium | Medium | Design the pilot to include diverse schools: different fee levels, different geographies (urban, peri-urban, semi-rural), different sizes, and different school types (private, public). If the product only works in one segment, that's useful to know early. Build the rollout playbook based on what worked in the pilot, not on assumptions about what should work everywhere. |
